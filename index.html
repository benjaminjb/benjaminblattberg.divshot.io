Projects

ShareCare

— a Rails-based app for collaborative caretaking

ShareCare was developed to solve the problem of how several people could arrange and communicate about caretaking tasks. (Essentially: how could my girlfriend and I keep track of what our puppy needed.)

With a Rails core, ShareCare allows users to sign in securely and manage both their "caring teams" and the tasks for those teams, utilizing RESTful routing and CRUD methods for several models.

Tech stack: Ruby, Rails, JavaScript, jQuery, HTML, CSS, and Foundation; with an emphasis on AJAX calls and transactions scripts.

Shush!

— a Google Chrome extension for blocking tweets and avoiding spoilers

Shush! was developed to allow people to go to Twitter without fear of spoilers. Rather than block or mute people entirely (and thus miss on all their tweets) or rely on some pre-set words to avoid, Shush! allows users to input words to avoid and will block tweets with those words.

Built entirely with standard JavaScript and jQuery—and fully using several of the Google chrome.extension.* API—Shush! remembers lists of blocked words and continually processes tweets as they appear on the screen.

Tech stack: JavaScript (including Mutation Observers), jQuery, HTML, CSS, and Google chrome.extension.* API; with an emphasis on preserving good design principles that adhere to the particular architecture of Chrome extensions.

(For more on the process, see my code blog: general discussion on Chrome extensions and more technical discussion.)

Available on the Google Chrome Store

Efficient Errands

— a Sinatra-based app for planning your errands efficiently by finding the shortest route

For a two-day hackathon at MakerSquare, less than midway through the program, three other developers and I planned, designed, and executed this app that allows users to enter addresses and then returns an efficiently ordered route taking in all of those addresses.

My role was (1) to create the algorithm that would solve that Traveling Salesman Problem (though in the time given, we decided to use Google Map's built-in Waypoints algorithm); and (2) to assist in user experience design.

Tech stack: Ruby, Sinatra, JavaScript, jQuery, HTML, CSS, Google Maps API.

(For more on the process, read my blog posts on our hackathon: day one and day two.)
